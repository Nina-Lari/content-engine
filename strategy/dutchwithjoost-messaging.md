## The Core Differentiator

### Two approaches to learning Dutch

| Memorization-based courses | Dutchwithjoost |
| -------------------------- | -------------- |
| Learn through memorizing vocabulary and grammar rules | Learn through practicing conversations on everyday topics |
| You pass by recognizing correct answers | You pass because you can produce Dutch yourself |
| Works well for people who want to pass quickly | Works well for people who want to use Dutch after passing |

Both approaches can help you pass the exam. They're designed for different outcomes. Memorization-based courses focus on exam preparation. Dutchwithjoost focuses on learning Dutch you can use, with the exam as a natural result.

### What completion means in Dutchwithjoost

A lesson is complete when you can have conversations on the topic, not when you've finished the practice exercises. The conversation adapts to what you say, ask, and answer. It's not repeating a script. It's using what you've learned in a dynamic exchange.

This means you're building more than vocabulary. You're learning sentence structure, how to express yourself in specific situations, and grammar through use. Reading, listening, writing, and speaking develop together because every lesson practices all of them.

### Why this matters

The inburgering exam tests both recognition AND production. The speaking and writing exams require you to produce Dutch, not just recognize correct answers. Dutchwithjoost trains production from the start, so the exam tests skills you've already practiced.

## How to Describe What Happens in a Lesson

### The two-phase structure

**Phase 1: Practice**
Each lesson is built around an everyday scenario (ordering at a restaurant, making a doctor's appointment, introducing yourself). You practice a scripted conversation in small chunks:

- Read the Dutch and say it aloud
- Listen to the Dutch and repeat it
- Write the sentence yourself

You do all three for each sentence before moving to the next. This builds reading, listening, writing, and speaking together, not as separate skills to check off.

The scenario topics are also the KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society) topics, so you learn Dutch culture as you learn the language.

**Phase 2: Conversation**
After enough practice, you have a conversation with Joost on the same topic. This is NOT repeating the script. It's a new conversation every time. The flow changes based on what you say, ask, and answer.

Joost:

- Stays in the scenario you practiced
- Speaks only at your level (uses vocabulary you've already learned)
- Asks questions that prompt you to use the words yourself
- Corrects gently by showing better phrasing, not lecturing

The lesson completes when you can have conversations on the topic. This means you've demonstrated you can use the vocabulary, not just recognize it.

### What you actually learn

Through this process, you're building:

- **Vocabulary** — words you can actually use, not just recognize
- **Sentence structure** — how to put words together in Dutch
- **Grammar** — picked up through use, not memorized as rules
- **Situational communication** — how to say things in specific contexts
- **All five exam skills** — reading, listening, writing, speaking, and KNM together

### Approved phrasings

Use these:

- "You practice the vocabulary, then use it in conversation"
- "Lessons complete when you can have conversations on the topic"
- "Joost speaks at your level and only uses words you've already practiced"
- "Each lesson teaches you to handle an everyday situation in Dutch"
- "The conversation changes based on what you say. It's not repeating a script."
- "You learn all five exam parts together, not as separate modules"

Avoid these:

- "Use each word in sentences before moving on" (too vague, doesn't land)
- "Production-based learning" (jargon)
- "Active recall" (jargon)
- "Spaced repetition" (jargon, unless explaining the method)
- "Real conversations" (implies human conversation partner)

## How to Describe Joost (the AI Tutor)

### What Joost does

Joost is an AI conversation partner who:

- Has conversations with you in Dutch at your current level
- Only uses vocabulary you've already practiced in that lesson
- Asks questions that prompt you to respond (not just listen)
- Corrects your grammar by modeling better phrasing
- Gives feedback on pronunciation

### The key insight (from real user)

> "It's like speaking to somebody who is good in Dutch but only at my level."
> — Nina, Dutchwithjoost user

This solves the "Dutch Partner Problem": native speakers can't slow down enough to match your level. They learned Dutch as children and can't explain why things work the way they do. They're not teachers. Joost matches your level and explains when needed.

### Approved phrasings

Use these:

- "Joost speaks at your level"
- "Joost only uses words you've already practiced"
- "You can practice whenever you want, as much as you want"
- "You can make mistakes without anyone watching or switching to English"

Avoid these:

- "AI tutor" without explanation (sounds cold)
- "Chatbot" (sounds basic)
- "Unlimited AI conversations" (sounds like a feature, not a benefit)

## Competitor Positioning

### General principle

Describe what each approach does. Do not explain why their approach is inferior or "easy to build." Let readers decide which fits their goals.

### InburgeringOnline

**What they do:**

- Memorization-based course with video lessons and mock exams
- Covers all exam components
- Subscription pricing (€14.95-49.95/month)
- Established track record (4.4 stars Trustpilot)

**Fair comparison:**

- InburgeringOnline trains you to recognize correct answers and pass the exam format
- Dutchwithjoost trains you to produce Dutch in conversation

**Do not say:**

- "Just memorization" (dismissive)
- "Cheat sheet to the exam" (even though Nina said this — too harsh for published content)
- "Easy to build" or "cheaper to scale" (talking down)
- That they have more track record than us (self-undermining)
- That we are "newer" (self-undermining)

### Classroom courses

**What they do:**

- Scheduled sessions with a teacher
- Social environment with other learners
- Typically €500-2,000 for A2
- Limited individual speaking time (12 students, 1 teacher)

**Fair comparison:**

- Classroom courses provide social accountability and a human teacher
- Dutchwithjoost provides unlimited speaking practice on your own schedule

**Do not say:**

- "Expensive" as a judgment (state the price range, let readers judge)
- "Rigid schedules" (say "fixed schedules")
- "You only get a few minutes of speaking time" (say "speaking time is divided among students")

### Generic AI tools (ChatGPT, etc.)

**What they do:**

- Unlimited conversation practice
- No curriculum or structure
- No exam-specific preparation
- Free or low cost

**Fair comparison:**

- Generic AI tools provide open-ended conversation practice
- Dutchwithjoost provides structured lessons that cover what you need for the exam, with conversation practice built in

**Do not say:**

- That AI alone is enough to pass (self-defeating — see the quote we removed)
- That generic AI is "random" (say "unstructured")

## Claims We Cannot Make

### About the exam

- Do NOT say "the exam only tests recognition" — it tests both recognition AND production
- Do NOT say "the speaking exam is the hardest" as absolute fact — say "many people find the speaking exam challenging"

### About competitors

- Do NOT say their courses are "easy to make" or "cheaper to scale"

### About speed

- Do NOT say we are faster or slower than alternatives
- Do NOT say memorization is "faster for passing" (implies we are slower)

- Do NOT say "exercises" or "clicking through screens" as negatives — this talks down on other courses

## The Exam Pass Guarantee

### The message

We're confident in the course. If you complete all 43 lessons and don't pass the A2 inburgering exam, we refund you.

This is the core message: **confidence backed by action**. We believe that if you finish the course, you'll pass — and we put money behind that belief.

### How to mention it in content

**In BOFU content (comparisons, pricing discussions):**
"Dutchwithjoost offers an exam pass guarantee. Complete all lessons, and if you don't pass, you get refunded."

**In MOFU content (educational articles):**
Brief mention only: "We're confident enough in the course to offer an exam pass guarantee. Complete all lessons, and if you don't pass, you get refunded."

**In TOFU content (thought leadership):**
Do not mention. Keep the focus on education, not sales.

### What NOT to include in articles

- The specific 1/5 refund per failed part calculation (too detailed for articles)
- Eligibility requirements (save for the guarantee page)
- 60-day contact window (save for the guarantee page)

Keep it simple: confidence + guarantee + complete the course.

## Tone and Voice Reminders

- Write like an expert talking to a peer, not a brand talking to a prospect
- Specificity beats abstraction
- Show, don't label ("the lesson completes when you've used the vocabulary" not "our innovative approach")
- Thread value props naturally through content — never isolate them into a "Why Dutchwithjoost" section
- If advice would help someone who chooses a competitor, it's genuine advice. If it only helps us, rewrite it.

## Resolved Decisions

These questions have been answered and should guide all content:

1. **One-sentence description**: Use the outcome-focused version. "Real conversations" replaced with "practical conversations" to avoid implying human partner.

2. **User quotes**: Can be used with attribution like "One learner described..." or "As one user put it..."

3. **Exam Pass Guarantee**: Mention in BOFU and MOFU content. Focus on confidence: "We're confident enough to offer a guarantee." Don't include the 1/5 refund calculation in articles.

4. **Pricing in comparison articles**: OK to include.

5. **Naming competitors**: OK to name InburgeringOnline directly in comparison articles.

6. **Competitor framing**: Describe what their approach does neutrally. "Memorization-based courses" is OK. Don't say "clicking through screens" or imply their approach is lazy/easy.
