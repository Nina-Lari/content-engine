# Competitive Landscape Template

> Derived from your master knowledgebase. Be honest about competitor strengths — dishonest battlecards produce dishonest content. If your sales team can't use this with a straight face in front of a well-informed buyer, it's not honest enough. Repeat the competitor section for each major competitor (aim for 3-5). ## Market Overview

Before diving into individual competitors, set the context.

### Market Categories
What categories does your product compete in? Most products span multiple categories.

1. [Example: Travel Risk Management — we compete on intelligence, not logistics]
2. [Example: Threat Intelligence Platforms — we compete on accessibility, not depth for analysts]
3. [Example: ...]

### Competitive Landscape Summary
In 3-5 sentences, describe the overall competitive landscape. Who are the major players, what are the key battlegrounds, and where is the market heading?

[Example: "The travel risk / threat intelligence space is split between legacy enterprise providers (expensive, comprehensive, slow to innovate) and point solutions (cheap, narrow, often just repackaging government data). The gap is in the mid-market: companies that need real intelligence but can't justify a $200K+ annual contract or dedicate a full-time analyst to a platform. That's where we play. The market is consolidating around platforms that combine data with workflow — standalone data feeds are losing to integrated solutions."]

### Company and Product

### Positioning (How They Describe Themselves)

Summarize how this competitor positions themselves in their own words. Pull from their website, sales materials, and conference presentations.

[Example: "International SOS positions as the 'world's leading medical and security risk services company.' Their messaging centers on a combination of data, technology, and human response — they emphasize that they have boots on the ground in 1,000+ locations. They sell peace of mind through comprehensive coverage."]

### Strengths (Be Honest)

List genuine strengths. If your sales team encounters these in competitive deals, they need to know what they're up against.

1. [Example: **Brand recognition** — when a CSO says "travel risk management," ISOS is the first name that comes to mind. We're fighting against a 30-year incumbent.]
2. [Example: **Response services** — they don't just provide intelligence, they provide evacuation, medical response, and on-the-ground support. We can't match this.]
3. [Example: **Global coverage** — they have physical presence in 90+ countries. Their local intelligence network is unmatched.]
4. [Example: ...]

### Weaknesses (Where You Genuinely Differ)

These must be real weaknesses that you can demonstrate in a competitive situation — not wishful thinking.

1. [Example: **Data latency** — their threat assessments are updated weekly, not real-time. In a fast-moving situation, a week-old assessment is a liability.]
2. [Example: **UX designed for enterprise SOCs** — their platform requires trained analysts to operate. A 3-person security team will struggle to get value without dedicated onboarding.]
3. [Example: **Cost** — their minimum annual commitment prices out most mid-market companies. Below 2,000 employees, the per-head cost becomes prohibitive.]
4. [Example: ...]

### Positioning Gaps

What do they NOT cover that you do? These are your entry points in competitive deals.

1. [Example: **Speed to insight** — they offer comprehensive reports, but nothing that delivers a risk score in under 60 seconds for ad-hoc location queries.]
2. [Example: **Self-serve model** — everything runs through account managers. No API, no self-serve trial, no product-led motion.]
3. [Example: ...]

### Common Objections When They're in the Deal

What do prospects say when they're comparing you to this competitor? Write these as actual quotes.

1. [Example: "We've been with ISOS for years — switching feels risky when employee safety is on the line."]
2. [Example: "ISOS gives us response services too. You only do intelligence."]
3. [Example: "Our procurement team already has an ISOS contract — adding another vendor is more work."]

**How to handle each**:
1. [Example: "Don't position as a replacement. Position as an intelligence layer that makes their ISOS investment work harder — they get better pre-trip briefings, and ISOS handles response."]
2. [Example: "Acknowledge this directly: 'You're right — we don't do evacuation or medical response, and we never will. What we do is make sure you need those services less often by catching threats earlier.'"]
3. [Example: "Offer a pilot alongside ISOS. Let them compare intelligence quality side by side for 90 days."]

### Battlecard Summary

2-3 key talking points a salesperson can use in a competitive deal.

1. [Example: "ISOS is the gold standard for response. We're the gold standard for intelligence. The smartest security teams use both — ISOS for when things go wrong, and us for making sure fewer things go wrong in the first place."]
2. [Example: "Ask the prospect: 'How long does it take you to get an ad-hoc risk assessment from ISOS today?' If the answer is more than an hour, that's your opening."]
3. [Example: "If they're locked into an ISOS contract, don't fight it. Offer a 90-day intelligence pilot that supplements their existing setup. Let the data quality speak for itself."]
