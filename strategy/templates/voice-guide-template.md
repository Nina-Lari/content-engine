## Style Rules

Specific, enforceable rules about how content should be written. These should be clear enough that an editor can check a piece of content against them.

### Sentence Structure
- [Example: "Lead with the conclusion. Don't build up to the point — start with it."]
- [Example: "Keep sentences under 20 words on average. Vary length for rhythm, but if a paragraph averages over 25 words per sentence, it needs editing."]
- [Example: "Use one idea per paragraph. If a paragraph has more than one main point, split it."]

### Formatting Preferences
- [Example: "Use headers liberally. No section of content should go more than 3 paragraphs without a visual break."]
- [Example: "Bullet points for lists of 3+ items. Don't use bullets for 1-2 items — that's a paragraph."]
- [Example: "Bold key phrases for scannability, but no more than one bold phrase per paragraph."]

### Technical Language
- [Example: "Use industry terms when writing for practitioners — don't dumb things down. But always define terms on first use for general-audience content."]
- [Example: "Never use acronyms without defining them first, even common ones like ICP or SOC."]

### Numbers and Data
- [Example: "Always lead with the most impressive number. '85% reduction' is better than 'reduced from 40 hours to 6 hours.'"]

## Anti-Patterns

Specific patterns, phrases, and constructions that your content should never use. Be as specific as possible — examples are more useful than abstract guidance.

### Structural Anti-Patterns
- [Example: "Never open a LinkedIn post with a question. Questions feel engagement-bait. Lead with a statement or insight."]
- [Example: "Never use a bulleted list as the entire body of a post. Lists need context before and after."]
- [Example: "Never bury the value proposition after three paragraphs of setup. If the reader can't tell what you're offering by sentence two, rewrite."]

### Phrase Anti-Patterns
- [Example: "Never say 'In today's rapidly evolving landscape...' or any variation of it."]
- [Example: "Never say 'We're excited to announce...' — just announce it."]
- [Example: "Never use 'unlock', 'empower', 'drive', or 'leverage' as verbs."]
- [Example: "Never say 'best-in-class', 'world-class', 'industry-leading', or 'cutting-edge.'"]
- [Example: "Never use 'at the end of the day' or 'the bottom line is' as transitions."]

### Tone Anti-Patterns
- [Example: "Never be self-deprecating about the product. Confidence, not arrogance."]
- [Example: "Never talk down to the reader or explain things they obviously already know."]
- [Example: "Never use humor that requires explaining — if the joke needs a setup paragraph, cut it."]

## Example Rewrites

**After** (in your voice):
> [Example: "Our new platform is live. It scores any location on Earth in under 60 seconds, pulling from 25,000+ sources. If your current risk assessments take longer than your morning coffee, that gap just got a lot harder to justify."]

> [Example: "Removed jargon ('leverages,' 'cutting-edge,' 'best-in-class'), killed the passive voice, led with what's new instead of how we feel about it, and ended with a concrete comparison the reader can feel."]
