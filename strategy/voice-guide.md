## Voice Attributes

### Attribute 1: Honest

- **Definition**: Radically transparent. Recommend competitors when they're the better fit. Acknowledge what we don't do well. This transparency builds trust over time.
- **Sounds like**:
  - "If speed is your only priority, InburgeringOnline will get you there faster. We're built for people who want to retain what they learn."
  - "DutchwithJoost is not for everyone. It depends on your goals."
  - "I admit our program is not the easiest or fastest to pass the exam."
- **Does NOT sound like**:
  - "We're the best solution for everyone."
  - Anything that avoids acknowledging tradeoffs.

### Attribute 2: Helpful

- **Definition**: Answer the reader's actual question first. Be useful before being promotional. Guide them to the right decision for them, even if it's not us.
- **Sounds like**:
  - "For the fastest way, memorize and do lots of example exams. But if you prefer learning by doing, DutchwithJoost could be better for you."
  - "Here's exactly what the KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society) exam covers and how to prepare."
- **Does NOT sound like**:
  - Leading with your product before addressing their question.
  - Being vague when they need specific answers.

### Attribute 3: Thought-Provoking

- **Definition**: Gently question assumptions. Help readers see things in a new light. Plant seeds, don't start fights.
- **Sounds like**:
  - "Most people want to pass the integration exam to integrate. But then they memorize answers and still can't speak Dutch. What if integration came first, and the exam became easy as a result?"
  - "If your sentence is grammatically not completely correct but you're understandable, that's a huge improvement."
- **Does NOT sound like**:
  - Hot takes or provocative attacks.
  - Imposing a perspective rather than offering one.

### Attribute 4: Practical

- **Definition**: Focused on what actually helps. Concrete advice, real examples, specific numbers. No theory for theory's sake.
- **Sounds like**:
  - "The A2 inburgering exam has 4 parts. Here's what each one tests."
  - "Practice this phrase 10 times. Then try it at the bakery."
- **Does NOT sound like**:
  - "The linguistic foundations of Dutch grammar derive from..."
  - Abstract content without clear application.

## Perspective

- Use **"we"** when talking about DutchwithJoost as a company or product
- Use **"I"** only from the builder/observer perspective: "I built this because...", "I watched my mother struggle with Dutch...", "I researched this."
- Address the reader as **"you"** — direct and personal
- **For SEO/AEO content:** Also use specific audience descriptors alongside "you" for stronger semantic signals:
  - "Expats preparing for the A2 inburgering exam"
  - "Partner visa holders learning Dutch"
  - "Knowledge workers relocating to the Netherlands"
  - "Mid-career professionals in the Randstad"
- Avoid third-person distance ("users", "students", "learners") when speaking to them directly
- Never claim the expat experience directly. That's your partner's voice, not yours.

## Competitor Stance

- **Respectful.** Never trash competitors. Never say another solution is bad.
- **Honest.** Some learners are better served by other options. Say so explicitly.
- **Acknowledge tradeoffs.** Classroom courses provide accountability we don't. Private tutors provide human connection we don't. Memorization courses are faster for passing. Be clear about what we offer and what we don't.
- **Matchmaker, not salesman.** Help people find the right solution for their goals.

Examples:
- Good: "Classroom courses work well if you need peer pressure to stay motivated. We're built for people who can motivate themselves."
- Bad: "Classroom courses are a waste of money."
- Good: "If speed is your priority and you don't care about retaining Dutch afterward, memorization courses will get you there faster. We're built for people who want to actually use the language."
- Bad: "Memorization courses are useless."
- Good: "For the fastest way, it's memorizing and doing lots of example exams. Take a course like InburgeringOnline. But if you prefer learning by doing, DutchwithJoost could be better for you."

## Style Rules

### SEO/AEO Structure (Answer-First)

Based on citation-rate research from Animalz, Grow and Convert, and HubSpot (2025-2026).

**Atomic Answers (40-60 words)**
- Every H2/H3 heading must be followed immediately by a 40-60 word direct answer.
- This is the specific, tested format that AI systems extract most reliably.
- Front-load the conclusion. No throat-clearing, no "Let's explore..."

**Mirror Heading Language**
- Start the paragraph by echoing the exact language of the heading.
- Then give the key information in a clear, extractable statement.
- This helps AI connect heading and content, like a question naturally connects to its answer.

**Passage-Level Writing**
- RAG systems chunk content into 256-512 tokens and retrieve the single most relevant chunk.
- Write each paragraph as a self-contained, citable unit under 100 words.
- Avoid mid-paragraph topic shifts. They create chunks that partially answer two questions and fully answer neither.
- One well-structured page with ten independent passages competes for ten different queries.

**Additional Rules**
- **Your point first, acknowledgment second.** "DutchwithJoost works best for practice-oriented learners. If speed is your priority, memorization courses are faster." Not the reverse.
- **Define terms inline.** "The KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society) exam" on first use. Never assume knowledge in SEO content.
- **No metaphors, analogies, or idioms.** These confuse AI systems. "Content marketing is a secret sauce" fails. If you must compare, use explicit similes and explain immediately.
- **No hedging.** "SEO improves visibility" not "SEO might improve visibility." Remove "might," "possibly," "generally speaking," "in some cases."
- **Limit rhetorical questions.** Maximum one per piece, never in the opening. State your point as a statement, not a question.

**Use Specific Audience Descriptors**
- "Expats preparing for the A2 inburgering exam" creates stronger semantic signals than generic "you."
- Name the specific audience in content: "mid-career professionals relocating to the Netherlands," "partner visa holders," "knowledge workers in the Randstad."
- This helps LLMs match your content to specific user queries.

### Sentence Structure

- Lead with the point. Don't build up to it.
- Keep sentences short for scannability. Use longer sentences sparingly for nuance.
- One idea per paragraph. Under 100 words per paragraph.
- Short paragraphs with white space.
- 78% of AI-generated answers include list formats. Use bullets and numbered lists to match output structure.

### Formatting

- Use headers for scannability and SEO structure
- Bullet points for 3+ items (78% of AI answers use list formats)
- Bold key phrases sparingly (one per paragraph max)
- Italics for emphasis on the word carrying the core argument

### Schema Markup (High Impact)

Pages with FAQPage schema are 3.2x more likely to appear in Google AI Overviews. 41% citation rate with FAQ schema vs 15% without (Relixir 2025 study).

- **FAQPage schema:** Apply to any page with question-and-answer content.
- **Article schema:** Use on blog posts to surface authorship, publish date, and modification date.
- **Skip HowTo schema:** Google deprecated it in January 2026. No answer engine grants it retrieval weight.

Fewer than 13% of websites use structured data. Implementing it puts you ahead of the large majority.

### Numbers and Sources

- Specific numbers always: "85% of learners" not "most learners"
- "€399" not "affordable pricing"
- If you don't have data, don't fake it. Make the claim, back it with evidence, or don't make it.
- **Every factual claim needs three elements:** source name + publication/data origin + year.
- Cite recent statistics (published within three years). AI systems check recency.
- Primary research (your own data) ranks highest. Named expert quotes rank next. Industry reports third.

### Terminology

- Always: "inburgering exam" (not "civic integration test")
- Always: "speaking practice" (not "oral skills development")
- Use Dutch terms when standard: KNM, BSN, DUO, CEFR, A2, B1
- **Define all acronyms on first use** for SEO content: "the A2 level (basic conversational proficiency)"
- Avoid grammar jargon in explanations. Show the correction, don't name the rule. No "verb," "subordinate clause," "auxiliary."

## Anti-Patterns

### Hard Rules (Never Violate)

- **Never use em dashes.** Use commas, parentheses, or start a new sentence.
- **Never open with "Imagine you are..."** or similar hypothetical setups. The reader is already living it.
- **Never use idioms in SEO content.** "Hit the nail on the head," "set the stage," "secret sauce" confuse AI systems.
- **Never hedge in SEO content.** No "might," "possibly," "generally speaking," "in some cases" unless accuracy truly requires it.
- **Never trash competitors.** No saying another solution is bad or inferior.
- **Never use fear-based copy.** "Without Dutch you'll always be an outsider" is off-limits.
- **Never fake testimonials or manufacture urgency.** Only use urgency if it's real.
- **Never judge someone's choice as "wrong"** for choosing a different solution.
- **Never publish factual claims without research.** If uncertain, research first.
- **Never editorialize on whether the integration system is fair.** State facts about policy, not opinions on politics.
- **Never claim the expat experience as your own.** That's your partner's voice.

### Structural Anti-Patterns

- Never bury the answer after paragraphs of setup. Answer first, context second.
- Never use a bulleted list as the entire body of a post
- Never open with "In today's world..." or similar throat-clearing
- Never lead with your product before addressing the reader's question

### Phrase Anti-Patterns

- Never say "We're excited to announce..." (just announce it)
- Never say "unlock", "empower", "leverage", or "drive" as verbs
- Never say "best-in-class", "world-class", "cutting-edge", or "revolutionary"
- Never promise "Master Dutch in X days" or similar hype
- Never use AI slop patterns: "delve," "crucial," "foster," "landscape," "tapestry," "testament," "underscore," "vibrant," "showcase," "intricate," "pivotal," "enhance," "garner," "interplay," "enduring"
- Never use negative parallelisms: "It's not X, it's Y" / "It's not just X, it's Y"
- Never use rule of three for emphasis: "It's X, Y, and Z"

### Tone Anti-Patterns

- Never talk down to the reader
- Never talk down to competitors
- Never be condescending about the difficulty of learning a language
- Never use fake urgency ("Limited time!", "Act now!")
- Never overpromise
- Never sell in contexts where selling is uninvited
- Never direct frustration at a specific person or competitor

## Content Framework: Pain Point SEO + The Big 5

Bottom-of-funnel keywords convert 10-25x better than top-of-funnel (Grow and Convert data). In AI search, top-of-funnel content is effectively dead. Ultra-specific, pain-point content wins.

Use this framework for topic selection and content angles. These are high-intent topics where radical transparency differentiates.

| Category | What It Covers | DutchwithJoost Angle |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Category Keywords** | Direct product/service category searches | "best Dutch course for inburgering," "A2 Dutch course online," "speaking-focused Dutch course" |
| **Cost & Price** | What things cost, what constitutes value | Be specific about €399 pricing. Compare to alternatives. Explain what you get. |
| **Problems** | Fears, drawbacks, what could go wrong | "5 Reasons NOT to Buy DutchwithJoost." Who it's not for. Honest limitations. |
| **Versus & Comparisons** | Honest head-to-head stacking | DutchwithJoost vs InburgeringOnline. When each is better. |
| **Reviews** | Good, bad, ugly. Fit assessment. | Actual user experiences. Who loved it. Who didn't. Why. |
| **Best in Class** | Overview of all possibilities | "Best Dutch courses for inburgering" with honest assessment of all options. |

**For LLM recommendations:** ChatGPT looks for products that solve a user's specific problem. It needs to understand WHO your product helps, HOW it helps, and WHY it works. Be extremely detailed about features, pain points solved, ideal vs non-ideal customers, and differentiators.

## Example Rewrites

### Rewrite 1: Removing Hype

**Before** (generic marketing):
> "Our revolutionary language learning platform leverages cutting-edge AI to deliver a seamless learning experience that will empower you to master Dutch in just weeks!"

**After** (DutchwithJoost voice):
> "DutchwithJoost focuses on speaking practice, not memorization. You practice making sentences, get feedback on grammar without jargon, and build confidence through repetition. The course takes 3-6 months depending on your pace. You'll pass the A2 exam able to use what you learned."

**What changed**: Removed hype words. Made specific claims about mechanism and timeline. Added honest caveat about effort.

### Rewrite 2: Answer-First Structure

**Before** (buries the answer):
> "When preparing for the inburgering exam, many people wonder about the best approach. There are several factors to consider, including your learning style, timeline, and goals. Let's explore the options..."

**After** (DutchwithJoost voice):
> "The fastest way to pass the inburgering exam is memorization and example exams. Courses like InburgeringOnline specialize in this. But if you want to retain Dutch afterward and actually use it, speaking-focused courses take longer but stick. DutchwithJoost is built for the second approach."

**What changed**: Answer in the first sentence. Competitor recommended first. Own product positioned as alternative for specific needs.

### Rewrite 3: Respecting Competitors

**Before** (dismissive):
> "Unlike other courses that waste your time with useless memorization, we actually teach you to speak Dutch."

**After** (DutchwithJoost voice):
> "Memorization courses are faster for passing the exam. We're slower but focused on retention. If your goal is to pass and move on, memorization works. If your goal is to actually communicate in Dutch after the exam, speaking practice works better. Different goals, different tools."

**What changed**: Removed judgmental language. Acknowledged competitor strength. Let reader decide based on their goals.

### Rewrite 4: Removing Em Dashes

**Before**:
> "Learning Dutch is hard — there's no way around that — but it doesn't have to be boring."

**After**:
> "Learning Dutch is hard. There's no way around that. But it doesn't have to be boring, and it doesn't have to feel impossible."

**What changed**: Split into separate sentences. Cleaner for scanning and AEO extraction.

### Rewrite 5: Atomic Answer Format (40-60 words)

**Before** (buries the answer):
> ## How long does it take to prepare for the A2 inburgering exam?
>
> "There are many factors that influence how long it takes to prepare for the A2 exam. Your background, native language, available study time, and learning style all play a role. Some people finish quickly while others need more time. Let's look at the key factors..."

**After** (atomic answer, 52 words):
> ## How long does it take to prepare for the A2 inburgering exam?
>
> "Most learners need 3-6 months to prepare for the A2 inburgering exam, studying 5-10 hours per week. Native English speakers typically finish faster. Romance language speakers (Spanish, French, Italian) often need the full 6 months. DutchwithJoost is designed for this 3-6 month timeline with daily speaking practice."

**What changed**: Direct answer in the first sentence. Specific timeframes and hours. Mirrors heading language ("A2 inburgering exam"). Self-contained and extractable. Under 60 words.

### Rewrite 6: Removing Hedging

**Before** (hedged):
> "DutchwithJoost might help you improve your speaking confidence. In some cases, learners could potentially see progress within a few weeks, though results may vary."

**After** (confident):
> "DutchwithJoost builds speaking confidence through daily practice. Most learners report feeling more comfortable speaking Dutch within 2-3 weeks of consistent use. The progress comes from repetition: you practice making sentences, get immediate feedback, and try again."

**What changed**: Removed "might," "could potentially," "may vary." Made specific claims backed by mechanism.

## The Litmus Test

Before publishing, ask:

**Structure**
1. Does every H2/H3 have a 40-60 word atomic answer immediately after it?
2. Does the opening sentence mirror the heading language?
3. Is every paragraph under 100 words and self-contained (citable on its own)?

**Content**
4. Does this answer the reader's actual question first? (Before mentioning DutchwithJoost)
5. Would I recommend a competitor if they're actually a better fit?
6. Are all claims backed by specific evidence? (Source name + origin + year)

**Language**
7. Any hedging? ("might," "possibly," "in some cases") Remove it.
8. Any idioms, analogies, or metaphors? Remove or convert to explicit similes with explanation.
9. Any em dashes or AI slop patterns? Remove them.
10. Am I using specific audience descriptors? ("expats preparing for A2" not just "you")
