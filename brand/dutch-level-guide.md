# Dutch Difficulty Level Reference (CEFR A1 / A2)

The second half of the Dutch checker. The companion file `dutch-grammar.md` answers "is it correct?" This file answers "is it easy enough for our audience?"

**Our audience watches at A1.** They are early expats and beginners who can read a slow, simple line and feel the win of understanding it. The Dutch in our posts should be **as easy as it can be while still being natural**. A correct sentence that is too hard is a failed sentence: the viewer bounces, does not save, does not feel the "I understood that" hit that makes them follow. When in doubt, make it easier.

This file gives the agent a way to score any Dutch line as **A1, A2, or above**, and to rewrite anything above A1 down to A1 (or flag it if it genuinely cannot go lower).

---

## 1. CEFR A1 vs A2 at a glance

| | A1 (Breakthrough) | A2 (Waystage) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Can-do | Simple phrases for immediate needs. Understands slow, clear speech on familiar topics. Answers direct questions with short phrases. | Simple, routine exchanges of information on familiar topics. Connected sentences, not just isolated phrases. |
| Active vocabulary | ~500 to 1,000 words | ~1,000 to 2,000 words |
| Grammar | Present tense. Very basic sentence structures. | Adds the perfect/past tense and basic conjunctions (`maar`, `want`, `omdat`). |
| Sentence shape | Short, single-clause. | Short clauses joined by a simple connector. |
| Our use | The default. Everything we publish should land here. | Allowed sparingly, only when A1 genuinely cannot carry the meaning, and always paired with the English line. |

Reference points: a learner needs roughly 90 to 100 guided hours to reach A1, and another 90 to 100 (about 180 to 200 total) to reach A2. So an A2 line asks for roughly double the study time of the viewer we are writing for. That is the gap we are trying not to cross.

## 2. The three axes of difficulty

Difficulty is not one thing. A line gets harder along three independent axes. Score each one.

### Axis 1: Vocabulary

This is the axis that matters most for us, because a single unknown word can break comprehension of an otherwise simple sentence.

**A1 vocabulary is:**
- High-frequency, everyday core words: `ik`, `jij`, `gaan`, `hebben`, `willen`, `huis`, `werk`, `eten`, `praten`, `vandaag`, `goed`, `niet`.
- Concrete and physical, not abstract: `tafel`, `brood`, `trein`, not `mogelijkheid`, `ontwikkeling`, `gelegenheid`.
- Thematic A1 fields: greetings, family, numbers, time/days/months, food and drink, shopping, directions, home, basic feelings, the most common verbs.
- Cognates the viewer already half-knows: `start`, `probleem`, `pauze`, `lamp`, `restaurant`.

**Pushes a line above A1:**
- Abstract nouns (`-heid`, `-ing`, `-tie` endings often signal these: `vrijheid`, `ervaring`, `situatie`).
- Low-frequency or formal/written words where a common one exists (`gewoonweg` instead of `gewoon`, `dien­tengevolge` instead of `daarom`).
- Idioms and figurative language (`de kat uit de boom kijken`). A1 viewers read literally.
- Specialized or topic-jargon vocabulary.

**Test:** would a beginner four weeks into a course know this word? If not, swap it for a more common one or gloss it in the English line.

### Axis 2: Grammar

The grammar *structures* a sentence uses place it on the level ladder. Match the list, do not exceed it.

**A1 grammar (allowed freely):**
- Present tense (section 2.1 of `dutch-grammar.md`).
- Simple main clauses with V2 and inversion.
- Yes/no and question-word questions.
- Imperatives (`Probeer het.`, `Zeg het maar.`).
- Modal verb + infinitive (`Mag ik...`, `Kun je...`, `Ik wil...`). These are everywhere in A1 polite requests and are fine.
- Basic negation (`niet`, `geen`).

**A2 grammar (use only when necessary):**
- Perfect tense (`Ik heb het geprobeerd.`).
- Subordinate clauses with `omdat`, `dat`, `als`, and the verb-final order they force.
- Comparatives (`groter dan`, `net zo ... als`).

**Above A2 (avoid in our content):**
- Simple past of strong verbs used in narration, relative clauses (`de man die...`), passive voice (`wordt gedaan`), conditional (`zou ... kunnen`), `er`-constructions beyond the basics.

A reliable signal: **clause count.** One clause is A1. Two clauses joined by `en`/`maar`/`want` is high A1 to low A2. A clause that forces the verb to the end (`omdat ik ... woon`) is A2. More than two clauses is above our range.

### Axis 3: Sentence structure and length

Even with easy words and easy grammar, a long or densely packed sentence reads as harder.

- **Length.** Aim for **5 to 9 words** per Dutch line. A1 lines are short by nature. If a line runs past ~12 words, it is doing too much, split it.
- **One idea per line.** Our props show one phrase at a time. Each `nl` line should carry a single, complete, sayable thought.
- **Front-load nothing complex.** Long fronted phrases force inversion the viewer has to untangle. Prefer starting with the subject or a one-word time marker (`Vandaag...`, `Sorry,...`).
- **No stacked modifiers.** `de aardige oude Nederlandse buurman` is four words of load before the noun even lands.

## 3. The A1 target profile (what "good" looks like for us)

A line is on-target when **all** of these hold:

- Every content word is high-frequency and concrete.
- Present tense or a modal + infinitive. No perfect or subordinate clause unless the meaning truly requires it.
- One clause, 5 to 9 words.
- A consistent register (`u` or `je`, not both, see `dutch-grammar.md` section 7).
- It is a thing a real person would actually say in that moment (the `when` field).

Our existing "Keep them in Dutch" post is a good calibration set. Every line is A1 by this profile:

- `Mag ik het in het Nederlands proberen?` (modal + infinitive, 7 words, all common)
- `U mag gewoon Nederlands tegen me praten.` (modal + infinitive, formal register, common words)
- `Kunt u iets langzamer praten?` (modal + infinitive, 5 words)
- `Ik leer Nederlands. Ik wil graag oefenen.` (two short present-tense clauses, split into two sentences)
- `Hoe zegt u dat in het Nederlands?` (question word + V2, common words)

## 4. Red flags: signs a line has drifted above A1

Catch any of these during review:

1. **A word you would not teach in week one.** Especially abstract `-heid`/`-ing`/`-tie` nouns.
2. **A subordinate clause** (`omdat`, `dat`, `als`, `terwijl`) sending the verb to the end. A2 at best.
3. **Perfect or past tense** when present tense could say the same thing.
4. **Two or more clauses** in one line.
5. **More than ~12 words.**
6. **An idiom or figure of speech.**
7. **A construction from section "Above A2"** in axis 2 (passive, relative clause, conditional).
8. **A "smart" synonym** where the plain word exists (`gewoon`, `praten`, `willen` beat their formal cousins).

---

## 5. How to rewrite down to A1

When a line is flagged, fix it in this order. Stop as soon as it lands at A1.

1. **Swap the word.** Replace the rare/abstract word with a common, concrete one.
2. **Drop the tense.** Rephrase the perfect as present where the meaning survives.
3. **Cut the clause.** Turn a subordinate clause into a second short sentence, or remove it.
4. **Shorten.** Remove modifiers and filler until one clean idea remains.
5. **If it still cannot reach A1,** keep it at A2, make the English line carry the full meaning, and note in the runbook why the Dutch had to stay at A2.

### Worked examples

| Too hard | Why | A1 rewrite |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `Ik zou het waarderen als u wat langzamer zou willen spreken.` | Conditional `zou`, subordinate clause, 11 words, formal. | `Kunt u iets langzamer praten?` |
| `Het is voor mij een goede gelegenheid om mijn Nederlands te oefenen.` | Abstract `gelegenheid`, `om ... te` construction, long. | `Ik wil graag mijn Nederlands oefenen.` |
| `Omdat ik nog aan het leren ben, maak ik soms fouten.` | Subordinate `omdat` clause + `aan het`-construction, two clauses. | `Ik leer nog. Ik maak soms fouten.` |
| `Zou u de mogelijkheid hebben om mij te helpen?` | Conditional, abstract `mogelijkheid`, indirect. | `Kunt u mij helpen?` |

---

## 6. Output of the level check

For each Dutch line, the checker should record a small verdict the reviewer can scan:

- **Level:** A1 / A2 / above A2
- **Axes:** which of the three (vocabulary, grammar, structure) pushed it up, if any
- **Verdict:** keep as-is / rewritten to A1 / kept at A2 with reason

Anything marked "above A2" must be rewritten before approval. Anything at A2 needs a one-line reason in the runbook for why A1 could not carry it. The default expectation is that the whole post reads at A1.
