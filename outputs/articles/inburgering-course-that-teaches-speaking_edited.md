## Why people pass inburgering and still can't speak Dutch

You can pass the inburgering exam and still not speak Dutch because the exam asks you to produce the language, while most courses only train you to recognize it. If that sounds familiar to expats preparing for the A2 inburgering exam, you are not imagining it. Many learners notice that the people around them do not expect A2 to mean you can hold a conversation. The bar has quietly been set at passing, not speaking.

The integration exams are genuinely hard. DutchNews described sitting them as a "mission impossible" in February 2026. That difficulty is exactly why the memorization shortcut is tempting: drill the answer patterns, pass the test, move on. The catch is what "done" means inside most courses. A lesson is marked complete when you finish the exercises, which trains you to recognize the right answer rather than say it yourself.

## What "teaches you to speak" actually means

A course teaches you to speak when finishing a lesson requires you to produce Dutch out loud, not just recognize the correct answer. Most self-paced courses mark a lesson complete once you finish the exercises, which trains recognition. A course that genuinely teaches speaking is complete only after you have used the new words in conversation, which is what the exam and real life both ask for.

That gives you a yardstick with three parts. First, completion requires you to say the words, not just select them. Second, you meet vocabulary inside real situations and use it, rather than facing it as a list to memorize. Third, the practice meets you at your level instead of overwhelming you.

This standard is rare. Among self-paced courses, finishing the exercises is the near-universal definition of done. A course that holds back completion until you have actually used the words in a real exchange is the exception, not the rule. That single difference, *what counts as done*, is what separates a course that leaves you able to talk from one that leaves you with a certificate.

## 5 questions to ask before you buy any inburgering course

Before you buy any inburgering course, ask five questions that test whether it builds speaking, not just recall. They work for a classroom, a private tutor, an app, or a self-paced course. If a course answers all five well, it will leave you able to talk, whichever brand it is.

1. **Does finishing a lesson require you to say the words out loud, or just click through?** The exam asks you to speak, so the practice should too. A good answer: you cannot complete a lesson without producing the language yourself.

2. **Do you learn vocabulary in context, or as isolated lists to memorize?** Words learned inside sentences and situations transfer to conversation. Words memorized in isolation often do not. One learner described how much she disliked spending two hours memorizing a long list of words that did not connect to anything. A good answer: you meet each word inside a situation and use it.

3. **Is speaking unlimited practice, or one exam module you drill?** If speaking is treated as a single test to cram for, you get a few rounds and stop. If it runs through the whole course, you build the habit. A good answer: you can practice talking as much as you want, whenever you want.

4. **Does the practice stay at your level, or throw native-speed Dutch at you?** This is the same wall people hit when they try to practice with a Dutch-speaking partner, where the gap between native speed and a beginner makes real practice hard. A good answer: the course replies at the level you are actually at.

5. **Does it cover all five exam parts: reading, listening, writing, speaking, and KNM?** The A2 inburgering exam has five parts, and piecing them together from separate apps and books leaves gaps. KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society) is easy to forget until exam day. A good answer: one course covers all five.

Notice that none of these questions favor a particular brand. Any course that genuinely teaches speaking should pass all five. That is the point. Use them to judge every option, including the one you are already leaning toward.

## How the common options stack up

The main ways to prepare for the A2 inburgering exam are self-paced online courses, classroom schools, and private tutors. Each is the right call for a different goal. Here is where each one wins and what you trade for it, so you can match the option to what matters most to you.

**InburgeringOnline** is the most recognized self-paced option, built by an NT2 (Dutch as a second language) teacher and mapped closely to the exam. **Classroom schools** give you a human teacher, classmates, and a fixed routine that makes you show up. **Dutchwithjoost** takes a different approach to what counts as done. A lesson is complete when you have used the new words in conversation with Joost, not when you have finished the exercises. ## What a production-based course feels like to use

**The short version: a course built around using the language feels less like studying and more like rehearsing for real life. You practice each part of a lesson, then prove it in a conversation, and the lesson is finished only once you have used the new words yourself.**

Here is the loop. Each lesson is built around a real situation, like making an appointment or sorting something out at the pharmacy. You work through it in small pieces. For each piece you read it and say it, listen to it and say it, then write it, all three before moving on. The practice mixes in words from earlier lessons so they stay fresh.

Then you have a conversation with Joost. He stays in the lesson's situation but starts a fresh exchange, and he responds to whatever you actually say, so it is not a script you repeat back. The lesson counts as finished when you have used the new words in that conversation.

A few things make this work in practice. Joost only uses words you have already practiced and stays at your level, which keeps the practice usable instead of overwhelming. As one learner put it, he is not going to use the most advanced, native-level Dutch to reply to you. Because you use each word in conversation rather than meeting it on a flashcard, it transfers to real talking. The words that you learn, one learner said, you use, and that is what helps you communicate. And because the lesson is structured for you, your energy goes into the Dutch rather than into planning your own study. One early learner described it as not carrying the load of trying to engineer her own lesson. It is guided, so you just do it.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why can you pass the inburgering exam and still not speak Dutch?

Because passing and speaking are trained differently. The exam asks you to produce Dutch by speaking and writing, but most self-paced courses mark a lesson complete when you finish the exercises, which trains you to recognize the right answer rather than say it. You can memorize your way to a passing score and still freeze the first time someone actually talks to you in Dutch.

### Does the inburgering exam test speaking?

Yes. Speaking is one of the five parts of the A2 inburgering exam, alongside reading, listening, writing, and KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society). That is exactly why a course that never makes you talk leaves a gap. The exam and real life both ask you to produce Dutch out loud, not just recognize it.

### Which inburgering courses include real speaking practice?

Private tutors give the most one-to-one speaking time but cost roughly €2,000 to €6,000 to reach A2. Classroom courses include speaking, but in a class of around 12 your share of talking time per session is small. Self-paced courses built around using the language build speaking into the lesson itself, so a lesson is finished only once you have used the new words in a conversation.

### Is a classroom course better than a self-paced course for speaking?

It depends on your goals. A classroom gives you a human teacher, peers, and accountability, but it costs around €1,160 on average to A2, runs on a fixed schedule, and splits speaking time across about a dozen students. A self-paced course built around using the language gives you unlimited practice on your own schedule, which suits people who can motivate themselves.

### How much does an inburgering course cost?

Self-paced courses range from under €150 for the budget options up to €300 to €600 for InburgeringOnline. Classroom courses average around €1,160 to A2, and private tutoring runs €2,000 to €6,000. Dutchwithjoost is €399 one time. Prices change, so check each provider before you buy.
