## Section 1 — Why people pass inburgering and still can't speak Dutch (350w)

**Key argument:** The exam tests production (speaking, writing); most courses train recognition (clicking, matching, flashcards). You can complete the training and still freeze, because completing the training never required you to talk. Open with the insight, not setup.

**Evidence mapped:**
- **Originality nugget (lead):** a learner's friend passed the exam through a memorization course and "couldn't even pronounce inburgering" the day he received his passport. (insights.json, alternatives → InburgeringOnline context; anonymize the friend.) This is the whole article in one image — use it early.
- **Customer voice — the retention failure:** "I did it, I finished it. But I didn't feel like I retained anything, to be honest." (one learner, after a €300 A1 course.) (insights.json quotes)
- **Market's own low bar (make the reader feel seen):** "People don't even expect that at A2 you can have a conversation." (one learner.) (insights.json; also competitive-landscape "Normalized non-fluency.")
- **Named publication (Tier 4, use once):** DutchNews, "Enjoying Dutch integration exams: mission impossible" (Feb 2026) — external proof the integration exams are hard, which makes the shortcut-via-memorization temptation understandable.
- **Mechanism contrast (Tier 1):** most self-paced courses mark a lesson complete when you finish the exercises (product-design-evidence.md §3). That is recognition training. The exam and real life ask you to produce.

**AEO directive:** none here; the definition paragraph lands in Section 2. Open cold with the nugget.

## Section 3 — 5 questions to ask before you buy any inburgering course (450w)

**Key argument:** Hand the buyer a checklist that helps them regardless of which course they pick. Each criterion must be genuinely useful and applicable to multiple options — not reverse-engineered from one product's feature list (writing-quality.md "Advice sections" test).

**The five questions + evidence behind each:**
1. **Does finishing a lesson require you to say the words out loud, or just click through?** — the completion-criterion distinction (product-design-evidence.md §3). Useful test against any course.
2. **Do you learn vocabulary in context, or as isolated lists to memorize?** — customer pain: "two hours on one rule memorizing a million words that don't make sense together" (one learner, product-design-evidence.md §5 / competitive-landscape). Context vs. flashcards.
3. **Is speaking unlimited practice, or just one exam module you drill?** — competitors treat speaking as an exam component to teach to, not a proof mechanism (competitive-landscape InburgeringOnline weakness #4). Ask whether you can practice talking as much as you want.
4. **Does the practice stay at your level, or throw native-speed Dutch at you?** — the level-mismatch pain (native partner can't calibrate down; insights.json "impossible to learn"). Early-tester line: "He's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you." A good course meets you at your level.
5. **Does it cover all five exam parts (reading, listening, writing, speaking, KNM)?** — the five DUO components; piecing them from multiple resources is a real cost. (product-design-evidence.md §1 Coverage; competitive-landscape Feature Matrix "All exam parts.")

**Honesty check:** every question is one a reader could ask of a classroom, a tutor, or any app and get value. Do not phrase any question so only Dutchwithjoost passes. (writing-quality.md advice-section test.)

## Section 5 — What a production-based course feels like to use (350w)

**Key argument:** Show, don't label. Walk the loop: structured practice → conversation with Joost; the lesson is done when you've used the new vocabulary yourself. Feature → benefit → proof for each point.

**Evidence mapped (Tier 1 mechanism + Tier 2 voice):**
- **The two-phase loop (mechanism = proof):** practice phase (each unit read-and-speak, listen-and-speak, then write, all three before moving on; practice mixes new material with review) → conversation phase (Joost stays in the lesson scenario, creates a new conversation, prompts you to use the lesson's words, responds to what you actually say — not a script). (product-design-evidence.md §2.) Describe what happens; no jargon ("interleaving," "spaced repetition").
- **Level-matched replies:** Feature: Joost only uses words you've practiced and stays at your level. Benefit: the practice is usable instead of overwhelming. Proof (one learner): "He's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you."
- **Words in context:** Feature: vocabulary is used in conversation, not seen on a card. Benefit: it transfers to real talking. Proof (one learner): "The words that you learn you use… it helps me communicate."
- **Low cognitive load / guided:** Feature: the lesson is structured for you. Benefit: you spend energy on Dutch, not on planning your study. Proof (one learner): "I don't have the cognitive load of trying to engineer my lesson… it's just guided."

## Section 6 — Choosing the right fit for your situation (250w)

**Key argument:** Close goals-first, not product-first. Let the reader decide. Soft CTA only.

**Evidence mapped (honest fit guidance from competitive-landscape "How We Win" / "Where We Lose"):**
- **Pick a classroom** if you need peer accountability and a human teacher relationship. (Where We Lose: "needs peer pressure → classroom.")
- **Pick a tutor** if you want one-to-one human conversation and budget isn't the constraint.
- **Pick a cheap memorization course** if you just need the certificate fast and don't care about retention — and note honestly that for people who hate memorizing, learning by using can actually be the faster route (don't assume memorization is faster for everyone). (Where We Lose, nuanced bullet.)
- **Pick a production-based self-paced course** (like Dutchwithjoost) if you're self-motivated, want to actually speak on your own schedule, and want to walk away able to talk. (How We Win.)
- **Honest fit limit:** if you're already comfortable at A2 and need B1 now, an A1–A2 course isn't your next step. (Where We Lose.)

1. **Why can you pass the inburgering exam and still not speak Dutch?** — recognition vs. production; courses that complete on finished exercises train clicking, not talking. (§1, §2.)
