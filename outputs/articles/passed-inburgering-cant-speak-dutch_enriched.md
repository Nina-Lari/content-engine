## Section 2: Why Recognition-Based Learning Creates This Gap

**Word target**: 350

**Key argument**: Explain the cognitive science. Recognition is easier than production. Picking the right answer is not the same as generating the sentence yourself.

### Evidence Mapped

**Academic sources (citable with citation):**
- Swain's Output Hypothesis (1985): "Comprehensible input alone is insufficient for full language development. Production forces learners from semantic processing (understanding meaning) to syntactic processing (producing correct grammar)." Based on Canadian French immersion programs where students with years of input exposure still lacked grammatical competence in production.

- Hopman & MacDonald (2018): "Production-learning participants outperformed comprehension-learning participants on both vocabulary comprehension AND grammatical comprehension tests." Key insight: production practice builds recognition, but recognition practice does not build production.

- Karpicke & Roediger (2008): "Repeated studying after learning had no effect on delayed recall. Repeated testing produced a large positive effect on retention." Students' predictions of their own performance were uncorrelated with actual results.

**Named publication (citable):**
- Seidlitz Education: "Comprehension is not enough if the students are to demonstrate their knowledge through speaking or writing. Words need to travel from the passive lexicon into the active lexicon so we can retrieve them on-demand. This transfer does not happen on its own."

### AEO Directive
Structure as explanation with clear cause-effect. Include a standalone definition of recognition vs. production for featured snippet.

## Section 3: How Memorization Courses Are Designed (And Why)

**Word target**: 300

**Key argument**: Be honest about why courses use recognition-based completion. It's faster, cheaper to build, easier to measure. Learners feel progress. Not malicious, but optimized for wrong outcome.

### Evidence Mapped

**Customer intelligence (informs framing, anonymize for content):**
- Nina on Taalthuis: "It was actually very boring. I would spend two hours a day on it... a bunch of grammar rules that I had to memorize... no speaking at all. I did it, I finished it. But I didn't feel like I retained anything."
- Nina on InburgeringOnline: "It's cards to memorize. It's a cheat sheet to the exam."
- Nina on UVA course: "Between lesson one and lesson two, the jump of difficulty was insane... I didn't even, they would show it to you, then you have to repeat it, but it would be gone."

**Structural insight (from positioning):**
- Most courses measure progress by exercises completed: click through screens, match words to pictures, fill in blanks, pick correct options. Every exercise trains recognition. None train production.
- Progress measurement favors exercise-based courses: "80% of exercises completed" is easy to track. "Can form sentences using the vocabulary" requires testing actual production.

### AEO Directive
Do NOT sound dismissive of competitors. State structural limitations factually. Frame as optimization choices, not laziness.

## Section 5: What Production-Based Learning Looks Like

**Word target**: 350

**Key argument**: Describe what it means to require active production for completion. Lessons aren't done when you finish exercises. They're done when you've used the vocabulary in conversation.

### Evidence Mapped

**Academic sources (support the principle):**
- Bjork & Bjork (2011) "Desirable Difficulties": Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval practice feel harder during practice but produce better long-term outcomes. Learners often underestimate the effectiveness of difficult practice.
- Nakata (2017): 5+ retrievals produce significantly better retention than 1-3 retrievals. There is a threshold of retrieval practice needed for durable learning.

**Product positioning (present as one approach, not universal):**
- Dutchwithjoost approach: Lessons complete when you've used most of the vocabulary in conversation, not when you've finished exercises.
- Two-phase model: practice prepares (read/speak, listen/speak, write for each sentence), conversation proves (must use target vocabulary to complete).
- Practice at your level: Joost speaks at the lesson's difficulty level. Unlike native speakers, there's no mismatch.

**Customer intelligence (supports the pain of finding alternatives):**
- Nina on level mismatch with Dutch partner: "Your level of Dutch is native and mine is total beginner. It's hard to find the middle ground. I don't understand a lot of what she's saying."
- Nina on taalcafes: "Everyone comes with different levels. They're not teachers. So you're just mumbling together. You don't even know if what you're learning is correct."

### AEO Directive
Describe the approach without claiming it as universal requirement. Be clear this is how Dutchwithjoost works, not a universal standard.

## Section 6: How to Know If a Course Will Actually Make You Speak

**Word target**: 300

**Key argument**: Give practical criteria for evaluating any course. Applies to any course, including ours. Builds trust through objectivity.

### Evidence Mapped

**Evaluation criteria (from positioning + customer intelligence):**

1. **What counts as completing a lesson?**
   - Exercises clicked through = recognition focus
   - Demonstrated production required = production focus

2. **How much speaking practice is included?**
   - None, limited scheduled sessions, or unlimited?
   - Classroom: limited per-student time. Private tutor: expensive per hour. Self-paced: varies.

3. **When does speaking practice start?**
   - Speaking at the end = cram for it like any other section
   - Speaking throughout = production skills built from day one

4. **Does the practice match your level?**
   - Native speakers: level mismatch, can't explain grammar ("they learned it since they're little so they can't really explain why")
   - Other beginners: "mumbling together," no one corrects mistakes
   - Structured practice: matches current level

5. **Can you practice on your own schedule?**
   - Classroom: fixed times. Tutors: scheduling required. Self-paced: anytime.

**Voice guide reminder:**
- These criteria should be genuinely useful even if the reader chooses a competitor
- "Different goals, different tools"

### AEO Directive
Structure as a practical checklist. Self-contained answers for each criterion.
