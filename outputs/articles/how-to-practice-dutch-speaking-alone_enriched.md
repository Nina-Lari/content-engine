### Section 2: Method 1 — Shadowing (repeat what you hear)
**Word Target:** 300 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Explain shadowing technique: listen to Dutch audio and speak along simultaneously or with slight delay
- Benefits: trains pronunciation, rhythm, and muscle memory without needing a partner
- Resources: Dutch podcasts, YouTube videos with clear speech
- How to do it: start with slow content, increase speed gradually

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Research backing (speaking repetition):**
   > "Significant improvements in speech rate during third delivery compared to first. Reduction in number of hesitations. Improvements in mean length of run (longer uninterrupted speech)."
   — Boers et al. (2021), 4/3/2 Speaking Technique study

2. **Research backing (production effect):**
   > "Reading words aloud improves recognition memory compared to silent reading. Speaking creates stronger, more discriminable memory traces."
   — MacLeod & Bodner (2017), The Production Effect

3. **Practical validation:**
   Kaiwa blog ranks for this query with shadowing as a primary method. Technique readers can start immediately with no tools.

**Evidence Gap:** No specific Dutch shadowing resources cited. Consider mentioning Dutch podcasts or YouTube channels with clear speech.

### Section 3: Method 2 — Self-talk (narrate your day in Dutch)
**Word Target:** 250 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Explain self-talk practice: narrate what you're doing throughout the day in Dutch
- Example: "Ik maak ontbijt. Ik pak de koffie."
- Benefits: builds production habit, uses real vocabulary, zero scheduling required
- Tip: start with simple present tense, add complexity as you improve

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Nina transcript evidence (using words in context):**
   > "The words that you learn you use which is really useful because if I'm learning new words and I'm using them in a sentence and I know how in which context I'm using them... it helps me communicate."
   — Customer interview

2. **Research backing (output hypothesis):**
   > "Production forces learners from semantic processing (understanding meaning) to syntactic processing (producing correct grammar)."
   — Swain (1985), Output Hypothesis

3. **Common polyglot advice:**
   Self-talk is widely recommended in language learning communities as zero-cost production practice. No scheduling, no partner, no judgment.

### Section 4: Method 3 — Record and review yourself
**Word Target:** 250 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Use your phone to record yourself speaking Dutch, then listen back
- Benefits: hear your own mistakes, track improvement over time, build awareness of pronunciation issues
- Can combine with reading Dutch texts aloud or answering practice questions
- Note: uncomfortable at first, but effective

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Research backing (self-assessment):**
   > "Students improved speaking fluency through repetitions and self-assessment. Participants reported: 'I learned because I repeated.'"
   — Santos (2022), Speaking Fluency Through 4/3/2 Technique

2. **Nina transcript evidence (tracking progress):**
   > "Being able to track that effectively is important for me. It makes me satisfied with myself."
   — Customer interview

3. **Practical framing:**
   Recording yourself feels awkward at first, but hearing your own speech reveals gaps you cannot notice in the moment. This is the "noticing function" from language acquisition research.

### Section 5: Method 4 — AI conversation practice
**Word Target:** 350 words

**Key Arguments:**
- AI conversation tools: ChatGPT, Gemini, dedicated language apps
- Benefits: available 24/7, adjusts to your level, no judgment, unlimited practice
- How to use effectively: set up scenarios, ask for correction, practice specific vocabulary
- Limitations: doesn't match structured curriculum unless designed for it

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Reddit success story:**
   > "After 4 months with AI, I passed all exams first try straight 10s."
   — Reddit r/learndutch user

2. **Nina transcript evidence (judgment-free practice):**
   > "There is no memorizing which I really like... When I use Joost, I don't feel like it's effort. It's still effort, but it's like going to the gym for lifting weights versus playing a match of badminton that you like. You're still working out but you're having fun."
   — Customer interview

3. **Nina transcript evidence (level matching):**
   > "It's like speaking to somebody who is good in Dutch but only at my level, which is great."
   — Customer interview

4. **Market validation:**
   Growing category with mydutchai.com, SpeakPal, Talkio AI, LangBuddy.ai showing market demand for AI conversation practice.

5. **Shame/avoidance pattern:**
   > "You want to avoid the discomfort and so you avoid the situations and then you avoid speaking Dutch completely. And then you avoid people completely."
   — Customer interview

   AI eliminates this shame cycle by providing judgment-free practice space.

### Section 6: Method 5 — Structured AI courses with conversation requirements
**Word Target:** 300 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Production-based courses where conversation is built into the learning path
- Different from open-ended AI chat: vocabulary is structured, progress is tracked, completion requires actual production
- Position as a category, not just product

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Key differentiator:**
   > "Completion requires using vocabulary in conversation, not just finishing exercises."
   — Product positioning

2. **Research backing (retrieval threshold):**
   > "5+ retrievals produced significantly better retention than 1-3 retrievals. Benefits held regardless of posttest timing."
   — Nakata (2017)

3. **Nina transcript evidence (guided structure):**
   > "I like that in the latest version that it's guided. I don't have to think about what now what do I do now. You just do it and then you progress naturally."
   — Customer interview

4. **Nina transcript evidence (words in context):**
   > "In my past courses, I would just learn words here and there. I really struggle to see how they come together because I learn words isolated from the verbs. I just keep memorizing shit that doesn't stick because I don't know how to use it."
   — Customer interview

5. **Research backing (production vs comprehension):**
   > "Production-learning participants outperformed comprehension-learning participants on both vocabulary comprehension AND grammatical comprehension tests."
   — Hopman & MacDonald (2018)

### Section 7: How to combine these methods
**Word Target:** 200 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Practical schedule suggestion: shadowing during commute, self-talk during routine tasks, recording practice sessions, AI conversation for structured practice
- Start with 15 minutes/day and build up
- The key is consistency, not duration
- The best method is the one you'll actually do

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Nina transcript evidence (consistency tracking):**
   > "It's very clear how much effort I'm putting in every day, every session that I set my goal. When I reach it, it tells me... I can feel that I'm consistent."
   — Customer interview

2. **Research backing (spaced practice):**
   > "Spaced learning consistently produces greater retention than massed learning. Medium effect size (r = .34)."
   — Kim & Webb (2022), Meta-analysis

3. **Practical framing:**
   Layering methods creates multiple touchpoints throughout the day without requiring dedicated study blocks.

### Section 8: What changes once you start producing
**Word Target:** 100 words

**Key Arguments:**
- Production practice feels harder at first because it reveals gaps
- That discomfort is the learning happening
- Progress feels slow until suddenly you can speak in situations you couldn't before
- The goal is hundreds of low-stakes practice moments before high-stakes real conversations

**Evidence Assigned:**

1. **Nina transcript evidence (confidence building):**
   > "When I use Joost, I don't feel like it's effort... I don't have the cognitive load of trying to engineer my lesson or something. You just do it and then you progress naturally."
   — Customer interview

2. **Research backing (desirable difficulties):**
   > "Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval practice are 'desirable difficulties.' These strategies feel harder during practice but produce dramatically better long-term outcomes."
   — Bjork & Bjork (2011)

3. **Confidence framing from web research:**
   > "Walk into DUO confident because you've already had hundreds of conversations."
   — Positioning implication
