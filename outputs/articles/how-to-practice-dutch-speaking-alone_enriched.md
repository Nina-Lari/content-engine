## Section 2 — Method 1: AI conversation practice (the closest thing to a real partner)
**Word target:** 360 · **AEO directive:** lead method; this section should be the one most likely to be quoted in an AI overview. Open with a one-sentence answer to "what's the best way to practice speaking Dutch alone?"

**Key argument:** AI conversation solves all three walls at once — available anytime, no human to switch to English on you, and it can be held at *your* level.

**How to do it well (concrete, technique-first):**
- Tell the tool to stay at beginner/your level and not jump ahead.
- Speak your answers out loud even when you're typing — the point is production, not chat.
- Ask for corrections *after* the exchange, not mid-sentence, so you don't lose the thread.

**Honest trade-off (state it plainly):** a general tool like ChatGPT has no structure and no memory of your progress, so you have to engineer every session yourself — decide the scenario, set the level, track what you've learned. That's a lot of overhead for a daily habit.

**Feature → Benefit → Proof (structured option, woven not isolated):**
- *Feature (Tier 1 mechanism):* in a guided lesson the conversation partner stays inside the lesson scenario (ordering at a pharmacy, making an appointment, meeting neighbours), replies only at your level, and only uses words you've already practised. The conversation changes based on what you say — it's not a script. (product-design-evidence §2)
- *Benefit:* every exchange is usable. You're never decoding C2 Dutch or driving the lesson yourself.
- *Proof (Tier 2 tester, "one learner"):* "Joost knows your level. He's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you." (product-design-evidence §5; nina)
- *Concrete level-matching example to include:* the partner builds the conversation only from vocabulary you've already worked through, so a beginner gets a beginner conversation about the exact scenario they just practised — not a generic free-for-all.

**Value prop woven here (brief prop #1):** a practice partner that replies only at the lesson's difficulty, so every exchange is usable input you can respond to.

**Completion-criterion thread (brief prop #2 — concrete example):** a lesson isn't done when you've clicked through the screens; it's done once you've used that lesson's words in a conversation. Reader-facing phrasing only — do NOT expose "90% of vocabulary" / "2+ uses" implementation specifics, and do NOT claim "the only course that requires production." (product-design-evidence §3)

## Section 3 — Method 2: Shadowing (builds pronunciation and rhythm)
**Word target:** 280 · **AEO directive:** define "shadowing" in the first sentence (self-contained, snippet-ready).

**Key argument:** Shadowing is listening to a short clip of Dutch and repeating it immediately, copying the sound and pace. It's the most direct fix for pronunciation — the block many learners name first.

**How to do it:**
- Start with slow content: graded/beginner podcasts, YouTube at reduced speed.
- One or two sentences at a time, out loud, mimicking rhythm and intonation, not just words.
- Repeat the same clip several times before moving on.

**Honest trade-off:** shadowing trains mimicry, not spontaneous production. You can shadow perfectly and still freeze when you have to generate your own sentence. Pair it with a method that makes you produce original language (Methods 1, 3, 4).

**Evidence (Tier 2 — pronunciation as a named, lived block):**
- A freelance illustrator, four years in NL, calls Dutch "my nightmare to pronounce" and froze on her doctor's surname during a phone call: "speaking and pronounciation is... NNNO. This is something that my brain just cannot work around." (monika insights, quotes/pains)

**Light product tie (optional, Feature→Benefit→Proof):** the course lets you replay your own recording and compare it to the correct pronunciation, and shows which specific words need work — built-in shadowing with a reference to copy. (product-design-evidence §6, "can say" column only.) Keep this light; the section is technique-led. *Place the explicit record-and-compare feature in Method 3, not here, to avoid double-counting.*

## Section 4 — Method 3: Record yourself and play it back
**Word target:** 240 · **AEO directive:** lead with the one-line method, then the steps.

**Key argument:** Speak for a minute — describe your day, answer a common question — then listen back and mark exactly where it broke down. Hearing yourself surfaces problems you can't notice in the moment.

**How to do it:**
- Phone voice memos. A fixed set of everyday prompts you'll actually face.
- Compare your recording against a native clip of the same kind of exchange.

**Tie to the situations learners actually need (Tier 2):**
- Grocery store: an American expat's near-term goal is "so that I don't have to switch to English in grocery stores." (molly)
- The doctor: the illustrator who couldn't manage her doctor's surname on the phone. (monika)
- Small talk with clients: the same illustrator wants functional Dutch to win Dutch clients. (monika)

**Honest trade-off:** no outside correction, so you can rehearse a mistake until it's locked in without knowing. Pair with a method that corrects you (Method 1, or the write-it step in Method 4).

**Feature → Benefit → Proof (place the product's record/playback here):**
- *Feature (Tier 1):* replay your own recording and compare it to the correct pronunciation; the lesson flags which specific words still need work.
- *Benefit:* you get the "play it back" loop with a correct reference and a pointer to what to fix, instead of guessing.
- *Proof:* this is how the course's pronunciation step is built (product-design-evidence §6, "can say" column). Do NOT say "AI analyzes your speech" or "perfect pronunciation scoring."

## Section 5 — Method 4: Write it, then say it
**Word target:** 240 · **AEO directive:** define the method in sentence one.

**Key argument:** Draft a few sentences, then read them aloud and re-say them without looking. Lower pressure than real-time speech, and a genuine on-ramp for anyone stuck in the shame cycle. It bridges written competence into spoken production — which is exactly the gap for learners who are strong on grammar but freeze when talking.

**How to do it:**
- A short Dutch journal entry, then speak it.
- Reply to a text in Dutch, then say the reply out loud.

**Evidence (Tier 2 — the grammar-strong, speech-frozen learner):**
- A former tech worker can complete grammar exercises correctly but freezes the moment she has to speak: "I can only say: I have a green apple." → attribute to "an expat who's strong on grammar drills" / "one learner." (anna insights, quotes)
- Shame-cycle framing (why low-pressure matters): "Shame plays a big role when you don't speak a language. Everyone around you expects you to just know." → "one learner." (nina insights)

**Honest trade-off:** still not fully spontaneous. It's a bridge, not the destination — it moves your existing written strength toward speech, but you still need real-time practice (Method 1) to close the gap.

**Light product tie (optional):** the course's writing step "guides you to discover the answer instead of just showing it" — same bridge from written to spoken. Keep light. (product-design-evidence §6)

## Section 6 — Method 5: Narrate your day in your head in Dutch
**Word target:** 200 · **AEO directive:** define in sentence one; this is the "always-available foundation" method.

**Key argument:** The free, always-available base habit. Narrate what you're doing: "Nu maak ik koffie. De koffie is te heet." ("Now I'm making coffee. The coffee is too hot.") Low stakes, constant reps, and it builds the habit of reaching for Dutch *first* instead of defaulting to English.

**How to do it:**
- Narrate routine moments (cooking, commuting, shopping).
- When you hit a word you don't have, note it and look it up later — that gap is your next vocabulary.

**Honest trade-off:** no feedback at all, so use it to build *fluency of habit*, not to check correctness. It's the connective tissue between the other methods, not a substitute for them.

**Light evidence (Tier 2, optional):** the informal "absorb it by living in it" instinct learners already have (the illustrator "spends time with Dutch people to absorb vocabulary") — narration is the solo, deliberate version of that instinct. (monika workflow_reality)

## Section 7 — What doesn't work (and why)
**Word target:** 200 · **AEO directive:** this is the myth-busting section; structure as 2–3 tight claims, each self-contained.

**Key argument:** Be honest about popular advice that trains the wrong thing, so the reader stops pouring months into input-only habits and wondering why speaking didn't improve.

**The distinction (the article's spine):**
- "Just watch Dutch TV" and "change your phone to Dutch" build **recognition** — which most stuck learners already have plenty of. They don't build **production**.
  - Evidence (Tier 2): "My vocabulary is okayish, I understand a lot, but speaking and pronounciation is... NNNO." — understands fine, can't produce. (monika)
  - Evidence (Tier 2): the grammar-strong learner who can still "only say: I have a green apple." (anna)
- **SINGLE ACADEMIC ANCHOR (the one citation for this article):** state the principle in plain language first — input alone doesn't make you a speaker; producing the language is a separate skill you have to train — then anchor it once with **Swain's Output Hypothesis (1985)**: years of comprehensible input in immersion programs still left learners unable to produce correct grammar; output is what forces the leap from understanding to speaking. One sentence, one source. Do NOT stack Hopman & MacDonald or any other paper on top.
- "Just talk to your Dutch partner" fails on level mismatch (callback to Section 1, Nina quote — don't re-quote in full, reference it).

**Why naming this matters:** it protects the reader from spending months on recognition work and blaming themselves when speaking doesn't move.

## Section 8 — Building a routine you'll actually keep
**Word target:** 200 · **AEO directive:** close with a concrete weekly cadence the reader can copy; consider a short bulleted "sample week."

**Key argument:** Combine the methods into something sustainable. Consistency over intensity; output over input.

**Sample cadence (concrete):**
- **Daily:** narrate your day in Dutch (Method 5) + five minutes of shadowing (Method 2).
- **A few times a week:** one AI conversation (Method 1) + one recording you review (Method 3).
- **As needed:** write-it-then-say-it (Method 4) when a real situation is coming up.

**Close — thread the structured option (value prop #2 + brief's closing note):** for readers who don't want to engineer their own practice, a guided course decides the sequence for them so the only job left is to show up and speak.
- *Proof (Tier 2, "one learner"):* "I don't have the cognitive load of trying to engineer my lesson... it's just guided. You just do it and then you progress naturally." (nina)
- *Proof (Tier 2, "one learner") — consistency:* "It's very clear how much effort I'm putting in every session. I can feel that I'm consistent." (nina)
