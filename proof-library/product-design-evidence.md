## Why this is the strongest proof we have

Dutchwithjoost's most credible, most ownable evidence is not a research paper. It is two things: **how the course is actually designed**, and **what early testing shows**. This is first-party evidence (Google's "Experience" in E-E-A-T) plus product-mechanism specificity. A reader believes "here is exactly how a lesson completes" far more than "a 1985 study says output matters." Lead with this. Cite a paper only when one external anchor genuinely strengthens a single claim.

## Evidence type and honesty boundary

Two kinds of evidence live here:

- **Product mechanism** — verifiable design facts. The specificity *is* the proof. State as "Dutchwithjoost does X," never "learning requires X."
- **Experience** — feedback from early testing. Anonymize as "one learner" or "an early learner testing the course." Never use a name.

**We do NOT yet have:** pass-rate metrics, third-party case studies, Trustpilot reviews, or named testimonials. When a section needs one of these, flag it as an evidence gap. Never fabricate a number or a quote.

## 2. How a lesson works (the mechanism is the proof)

Each lesson is scenario-based: ordering at a pharmacy, making an appointment, meeting neighbors. The learner is preparing to handle a real situation, not memorizing a vocabulary list.

**Two phases per lesson:**

1. **Practice phase** — the learner works through the lesson in small sentence units. Each unit is practiced three ways in sequence: read and speak, listen and speak, then write. This interleaving (not all-reading-then-all-listening) moves the language into usable memory. Practice sessions mix new material with review of earlier content.
2. **Conversation phase** — after completing the practice sessions, the learner has a conversation with Joost. Joost stays in the lesson scenario but creates a new conversation, prompting the learner to use the lesson's vocabulary naturally. Joost speaks at the learner's level and only uses words they have already practiced. The conversation changes based on what the learner says. It is not a script.

**How to write this:** describe what happens, not the method name. Say "you read it, listen to it, then write it, all three before moving on." Do not say "interleaved practice," "spaced repetition," or "production-based learning" (jargon).

## 3. The completion criterion (core differentiator)

**A lesson is not complete when you finish the exercises. It is complete when you have used the lesson's vocabulary in conversation with Joost.**

This is the single most differentiating, most ownable fact about the product. Other self-paced courses complete when you have clicked through the screens. Dutchwithjoost completes when you can produce the language.

- Reader-facing phrasing: "Lessons complete when you can have conversations on the topic, not when you've finished the exercises."
- Reader-facing phrasing: "A word counts as learned when you've used it in conversation, not when you've seen it."
- **Do not** expose the implementation specifics ("90% of target vocabulary," "2+ successful uses," "3 practice sessions to unlock"). Too technical for content; also presents our design as a universal rule.
- **Do not** write "the only course that requires production." It violates a red line. Describe what we do; let the contrast speak.

## 4. Positioning gaps we own (use as the spine of differentiation)

1. **Active production as completion** — completing a lesson requires using the vocabulary in conversation.
2. **Practice prepares, conversation proves** — structured practice gives you the material; the conversation with Joost proves you can use it.
3. **All exam parts in one** — reading, listening, writing, speaking, and KNM together, not pieced from multiple resources.
4. **Unlimited practice without judgment** — no scheduling, no per-hour rate, make mistakes without anyone switching to English.

## 5. Experience evidence (early testing — attribute to "one learner")

These are first-party quotes from early testing. Use as anonymized proof. Strong substitute for case studies until real outcomes exist.

- **Level-matched practice:** "Joost knows your level. He's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you." Use against the pain that native speakers and partners can't calibrate down.
- **No memorization grind:** "There is no memorizing, which I really like, because I hated spending two hours on one rule memorizing a million words that don't make sense together."
- **Words in context:** "The words that you learn, you use. If I'm using them in a sentence and I know the context, it helps me communicate." (Knowledge base permits attributing this to "one learner.")
- **Low cognitive load / guided:** "I don't have to think about what to do now. It's guided. I don't have the cognitive load of trying to engineer my own lesson."
- **Engaging:** "I don't see time passing with Joost because I'm having fun and I'm engaged." Like the difference between forcing yourself to the gym and playing badminton: still a workout, but you enjoy it.
- **Clear progress:** "It's very clear how much effort I'm putting in every session. I can feel that I'm consistent."
- **Contrast with past courses:** earlier courses left this learner "more lost than in the beginning," with isolated vocabulary that didn't connect. Here it ties together into usable combinations.

## 6. Feature claims: what we can and cannot say

Map only the "can say" column into articles. This keeps enrichment inside the guardrails.

| Feature | Can say | Cannot say |

**Outcome claims we can make:** "prepares you for all 5 parts of the A2 inburgering exam"; "you learn all five exam parts together, not as separate modules"; "the exam becomes straightforward because you've actually learned the language"; "we're confident enough to offer an exam pass guarantee."

**Outcome claims we cannot make:** "you will pass" (guarantee has conditions); "faster than other courses"; "you will be fluent" (A2 is not fluency).

**DUO handling:** It is true that we are not on DUO's approved provider list and the course is not loan-eligible. Never imply the reverse (that we ARE approved or loan-eligible). But also never state the non-approval in published content — naming it reads as a credibility flag when the product is credible. When a learner on a municipality-funded/assigned track needs to be disqualified, frame it as "check your track first," not "we're not approved."
